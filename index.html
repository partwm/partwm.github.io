Anti-Sway Experiments

The Anti-Sway task includes both a demonstration video and corresponding quantitative plots to illustrate how oscillations are suppressed during motion.

Each plot group contains two subfigures: the upper plot shows the relative displacement Δpos of the robot end-effector along the motion direction (with the initial position as the origin), illustrating the trajectory from the start to the target position, while the lower plot shows the pendulum angle θ of the suspended payload over time, reflecting the induced oscillatory dynamics.

Before control begins, the robot performs a short random motion to generate an initial swing so that all methods start from comparable swinging conditions; the gray-blue shaded region indicates when the control policy starts executing.

The objective of the Anti-Sway task is to move efficiently toward the target while minimizing oscillatory motion. Small transient tilts are acceptable, but large back-and-forth oscillations should be avoided and quickly damped.

A successful Anti-Sway execution therefore moves efficiently toward the target while avoiding large back-and-forth oscillations. When reaching the goal, small residual angles or velocities are acceptable, but the oscillations should be quickly suppressed rather than left to decay naturally.

Abstract

While object manipulation is fundamental to real-world robotics, its learning remains challenging due to the high sensitivity to complex physical interactions involving friction and restitution. The difficulty is further amplified for articulated objects, whose motion is governed not only by external interactions but also by internal kinematic constraints and joint-related dynamics.

To achieve robust policy learning and generalization, we opt to learn a world model of 3D rigid-body systems, including articulated objects, and use it for model-based reinforcement learning. We propose PART-WM, a Physics-Aware Articulated World Model that enables efficient end-to-end joint identification of physical dynamics and kinematic structures from visual observations. Adopting differentiable physics simulation, PART-WM can be learned with only few-shot and task-agnostic physical interaction trajectories.

Further, PART-WM is learned with observational loss induced by Gaussian Splatting without requiring explicit state estimation. To bridge Sim2Real gaps, we transform the learned PART-WM into a group of physics-aware digital cousins via structured randomizations that perturb rendering, physical, and structural parameters, generating diverse yet physically consistent model variations.

Extensive evaluations in both simulation and real-world settings demonstrate that PART-WM, enhanced with physics-aware digital cousins, facilitates robust articulated object manipulation with effective Sim2Real transfer, outperforming state-of-the-art Real2Sim2Real methods.

Method

A. Real2Sim World Model Identification

(a) Rendering alignment: The robot captures multi-view observations in the target domain to estimate the rendering parameters α of 2D Gaussian Splats, providing a consistent visual representation of the scene and object appearance.

(b) Geometry and mobility analysis: From the reconstructed observations, the method recovers object geometry and estimates articulation-related structural parameters κ, including motion axes and reference points for movable parts.

(c) Differentiable joint system identification: Given task-agnostic interaction data, the method jointly identifies the physical parameters θ and the structural parameters κ by coupling visual observations with a differentiable constraint-based physics solver.

B. Sim2Real Policy Transfer

(d) Policy learning with physics-aware digital cousins: To account for residual uncertainty, the identified rendering parameters α, physical parameters θ, and structural parameters κ are perturbed around their estimated values to construct physics-aware digital cousins for robust policy training.

(e) Zero-shot transfer to the target domain: Policies trained across these digital cousins achieve improved robustness and can be directly deployed in the target domain without real-world fine-tuning.
